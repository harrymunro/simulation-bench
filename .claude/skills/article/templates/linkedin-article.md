# Template: LinkedIn Article

Long-form content for LinkedIn's article feature (not regular posts).

**Do NOT follow this as a fill-in-the-blanks template.** A LinkedIn article should feel like a longer LinkedIn post, not a polished essay. If you could submit it to a journal, it's too formal. If it reads like you wrote it on your phone on a train (but with actual substance), you're closer.

## Best rhetoric devices

- **Antithesis** for attention-grabbing contrasts
- **Anadiplosis** for narrative flow between sections

Use at most 1-2. They must be invisible. See [ANTI_AI_DETECTION.md](../../shared/ANTI_AI_DETECTION.md).

## Platform Considerations

LinkedIn articles differ from blog posts:
- **Shorter overall**: 800-1500 words (vs 1500-2500 for blogs)
- **No SEO pressure**: Focus on engagement, not keywords
- **More personal**: LinkedIn audience expects your voice, your story, your opinion
- **Feed competition**: Hook must be exceptional. You're competing with the entire feed
- **Social proof**: Personal stories and specific results resonate more than general advice
- **Conversational, not academic**: Write like you're explaining something to a smart friend, not presenting to a conference

## Approaches

### Approach 1: The personal story with a lesson

Something happened to you. You're going to tell the reader about it, and there's a lesson or insight in there somewhere. The story does the heavy lifting. The "lesson" part might just be a paragraph at the end, or it might be woven through the story. Don't feel like you need to spell out what the reader should learn. They'll get it.

This works well for: career moments, project stories, business decisions, mistakes you made.

### Approach 2: The thing you changed your mind about

You used to believe X. Now you believe Y. What happened in between? This is naturally engaging because it's honest and shows growth. Don't structure it as "before" and "after" with equal sections. The interesting part is usually the moment of change, so spend more time there.

This works well for: professional growth, methodology changes, opinion shifts.

### Approach 3: The framework or method you actually use

You've figured out a way of doing something. Not a theoretical framework. Something you actually do, in practice, that works. Share it conversationally. Include where it breaks down or where you've had to adapt it. "I do X, except when Y, and honestly sometimes I just wing it" is more credible than a perfect three-step method.

This works well for: productivity approaches, business practices, technical methods, pricing strategies.

## Anti-patterns

- **The polished essay**: If it has an introduction, a body with balanced arguments, and a conclusion with a call to action, it's an essay. LinkedIn articles should feel more like you're talking.
- **The numbered list of lessons**: "Lesson 1... Lesson 2... Lesson 3..." with symmetric length and formatting. If you have lessons, make them different sizes. One might be a paragraph, one might be a sentence, one might be three paragraphs with a tangent in the middle.
- **The story-lesson-story-lesson structure**: Where every anecdote is neatly paired with a takeaway. Real stories have multiple lessons or no clear lesson at all. Let some stories just be stories.
- **Manufactured engagement closers**: "What do you think? I'd love to hear your perspective in the comments!" This is the most recognisable AI pattern on LinkedIn. If you want to end with a question, make it specific and genuinely curious. Or just end.
- **Hashtag stuffing in the body**: Save hashtags for the post that promotes the article, not the article itself.

## Example

```markdown
# I Charged £200/Day. Now I Charge £1,200. Here's What Changed.

Three years ago, I was competing on price. Pitching against
consultants who'd work for lunch money. Winning projects that
left me exhausted and underpaid.

A client told me I was "good value."

That phrase haunted me. "Good value" meant cheap. Nobody hires
McKinsey for "good value."

---

## So I changed three things

Not my skills, those stayed roughly the same. Not my CV, not my
certifications, none of the things LinkedIn tells you to invest in.

**I stopped selling time.** "I'll optimise your warehouse" became
"I'll find £50,000 in annual savings." Same work. Different frame.
Clients stopped counting my hours.

**I started saying no.** This was genuinely terrifying. I turned
down a £30k project because it was the wrong kind of work.
My partner thought I'd lost it. But the projects I took after
that were better fits and, this is the part I didn't expect,
I did better work on them because I actually wanted to be there.

**I started publishing.** Not to build a brand or become a thought
leader or any of that. Just to have something to point to when
someone asked "why should we hire you?" Every article was proof I'd
thought about this stuff before their project landed on my desk.

---

## What actually happened

My pipeline got smaller. That was scary for about three months.

Then the projects got bigger. And the clients stopped haggling.
Someone told me I was "expensive" and I nearly hugged them.

I should be honest: I also got lucky with timing. The simulation
market got busier right around when I made these changes. Would
it have worked the same in a downturn? Genuinely don't know.

---

What's the hardest part of charging more? For me it was the fear
that I'd price myself out and sit by the phone. That didn't happen
but I understand why it stops people.
```

Note: the example doesn't have a tidy summary. The three changes aren't symmetric (one is a sentence, one is a paragraph with a tangent about his partner, one is two sentences). The ending is honest about uncertainty and asks a specific question without the "I'd love to hear your thoughts in the comments" formula.

## Variations

These are directions, not structures. Let the piece shape itself.

### Behind the scenes

Reveal something you don't normally share. What something looks like from the outside vs the messy reality. Don't structure it as a neat "expectation vs reality" comparison. Just tell both sides and let the contrast do the work. If the messy reality is more interesting (it usually is), spend more time on it.

### Lessons from a specific experience

Not "5 lessons I learned" with uniform sections. Start with what happened, and let the lessons emerge. Some might be explicit. Some might just be implied. One lesson might get three paragraphs because it's complicated, another might be half a sentence.

### A method you use

Share how you actually do something, with the caveats, the exceptions, and the parts where you improvise. The goal is usefulness, not elegance. If your method has a weakness you haven't solved, say so.
