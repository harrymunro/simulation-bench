# Template: Personal Story Post

## Best rhetoric devices
- **Anadiplosis** for narrative flow
- **Litotes** for humble understatement
- **Hyperbaton** for reflective moments

## Approach. NOT a fill-in-the-blanks template

Do NOT follow this as a rigid structure. Personal stories should feel the most natural of all post types. They should read like you sat down and wrote something because you were thinking about it, not like you ran a framework.

### Shape 1: Start mid-story
Skip the setup. "The client hated the model." Then back up and explain. Starting in the middle is how people actually tell stories in conversation, they lead with the interesting bit, then fill in context when you look confused.

### Shape 2: The slow realisation
Start with what you believed, let the story unfold, and let the insight arrive gradually. Don't label the lesson. Don't have a "THE TAKEAWAY" moment. If the story is good, the reader gets there on their own.

### Shape 3: The tangent-heavy version
Start telling one story and genuinely drift into a related thought. Come back. Or don't, sometimes the tangent IS the point and the original story was just the way in. This is extremely human and almost impossible for AI to replicate well.

### Shape 4: The admission
Start with "I was wrong about..." or "I spent [time] doing [thing] badly." The vulnerability is the hook. The story is how you figured it out. But, and this matters, you don't need to tie it up with a neat lesson. Sometimes "I'm still not sure I've fixed it" is the most honest ending.

## Example

```
Six months. That's how long I spent building a simulation
model that the client ended up hating.

Not because it was wrong, the numbers were solid, the
logic was tight, it matched their historical data within
2%. I was quite proud of it actually.

They hated it because it showed them things they didn't
want to see. Bottlenecks they'd been arguing about for
years, but in a way that made it impossible to keep
pretending the problem was somewhere else. I'd built
exactly what they asked for. Just not what they needed.

I nearly gave up on simulation after that project. Not
because the work was bad but because I realised technical
correctness doesn't actually matter if nobody wants to
hear the answer. That's a hard lesson when you've spent
your whole career optimising for correctness.

Now I start every project with "What decision will this
help you make?" and if they can't answer, we're not ready
to build anything yet. Sounds obvious but it took me a
failed project and a lot of awkward meetings to get there.

#Simulation #Engineering #LessonsLearned
```

## Anti-patterns to avoid

These are the AI tells most common in personal story posts:

- **"Three years ago, I..."** as the standard opener, time markers are fine but don't always lead with one. Start with the emotion, the outcome, the weird detail that stuck with you. "Three years ago" works once in a while, not every time.
- **The clean narrative arc**: Setup, challenge, turning point, lesson. Real stories are messier. The turning point might not be clear. The lesson might be ambiguous. You might still not fully understand what happened. That uncertainty is human.
- **Performative vulnerability**: "I failed, and here's what I learned" delivered smoothly and confidently doesn't feel vulnerable. Actual vulnerability has hesitation, qualifications, and moments where you're not sure you're telling the story right. If the failure sounds like a TED talk, it's too polished.
- **The universal lesson**: "Technical correctness is not the same as usefulness" stated as a clean aphorism. Real people learn messy, specific lessons: "I should have asked more questions in the first meeting" or "I think I was building that model to impress them, not to help them." Specific and slightly uncomfortable beats clean and quotable.
- **Every paragraph advancing the narrative**: Real storytelling includes dead ends, irrelevant details that you mention because they were part of the experience, and asides that don't serve the plot. If every sentence moves the story forward efficiently, it reads as composed, not remembered.

## Voice notes for personal stories

- Be genuinely vulnerable, not performatively. If the story doesn't make you slightly uncomfortable to share, it might not be vulnerable enough.
- Stories where you're NOT the hero often work best
- Specific details make it real (£8,000, six months, page 47, "that Tuesday meeting")
- You don't always need to end with something useful for the reader. Sometimes the story is enough.
- "I was wrong" is more powerful than "I was right"
- Litotes works well here, understate your successes
- Incomplete thoughts and genuine uncertainty ("I'm still not sure I handled that right") read as more human than clean resolutions
- Leave some things unsaid. The reader doesn't need every detail explained.
