# Template: Launch Announcement Email

## Purpose
Drive action for a specific offering. Convert interested subscribers.

**Do NOT follow this as a fill-in-the-blanks template.** Launch emails are inherently more structured and sales-y than newsletters, that's fine. But the structure should feel like natural persuasion, not a copywriting framework executed in order. Read [ANTI_AI_DETECTION.md](../../shared/ANTI_AI_DETECTION.md) first.

## Best rhetoric devices
- **Antithesis** for before/after contrasts
- **Tricolon** for listing benefits (but break the symmetry, see anti-patterns below)
- **Anaphora** for building momentum

## Loose Structure

A launch email generally needs to cover: what this is, why it matters, what's inside, and how to get it. But you don't need to cover all of these every time, and you definitely don't need to cover them in a fixed order.

The classic copywriting formula is Problem -> Solution -> Proof -> Offer -> Urgency. AI loves this sequence. Vary it:
- Lead with the offer ("The Simulation Bootcamp opens Monday") and then explain why it exists
- Lead with proof ("94% of the last cohort finished. That number surprised me too.")
- Lead with a story and let it build to the offer
- Skip urgency entirely if there's no genuine deadline

```
Subject: [Clear, direct, not a copywriting formula.
"The Simulation Bootcamp opens Monday" is better than
"The secret to mastering simulation in 90 days"]

[The core message. What's happening and why it matters.
Get to this fast, don't build up to it through three
paragraphs of problem framing.]

[Details, what's included, who it's for, what results
look like. Use bullets if you need them, but don't make
every bullet a mirror image of the others.]

[Social proof if you have it, specific numbers, quotes,
results. Keep it brief.]

[The ask. Clear, single CTA. Link or button, not both.]

Harry

P.S. [The human touch, an honest out, a personal note,
an offer to answer questions directly.]
```

## Subject Line Examples
- "The Simulation Bootcamp opens Monday"
- "Doors close in 48 hours"
- "Your invitation: Simulation Bootcamp 2024"
- "It's time"
- "quick update on the bootcamp"

**Warning on subject lines**: Launch email subjects are the highest AI-detection risk. "Unlock your simulation potential" and "Transform your engineering career in 90 days" are obvious tells. Keep it factual and direct. See the subject line guidance in [SKILL.md](../SKILL.md).

```
Subject: The Simulation Bootcamp opens Monday

In three months, you could be the simulation expert
your team relies on.

Right now, you're probably struggling with commercial

not what you want. Or watching tutorials that show toy
examples, not real problems.

The Simulation Bootcamp is everything I learned in ten
years of building simulations for clients, compressed

```

## Anti-Patterns

Things that make launch emails smell like AI:

- **Perfectly parallel benefit bullets.** "Master X. Achieve Y. Transform Z.". each starting with a verb, each the same length, each with bold formatting. Real bullet points vary in length and structure. One might be a sentence, another a short paragraph with context.
- **The Problem -> Solution -> Proof -> Offer -> Urgency sequence in exact order.** This is a known formula. Using it verbatim is an AI tell. Rearrange, skip sections, or combine them.
- **Tricolon lists that are too clean.** "Not theoretical exercises. Not academic projects. Not toy examples.". three parallel phrases with the same structure. Break the pattern: collapse two into one sentence, make the third longer, or just use two.
- **Manufactured urgency.** "Only 30 spots left!" when there's no actual cap. If the urgency isn't real, skip it. Readers can smell fake scarcity.
- **The formulaic subject line.** "The [adjective] way to [achieve dream outcome] in [timeframe]". this is ChatGPT's favourite launch subject. Be direct instead.
- **No P.S. line.** AI rarely generates P.S. lines unprompted. A genuine P.S. with an honest "reply if you're not sure" is one of the most human things in email marketing.

## Email Sequence for Launch

### Pre-launch (Day -7)
- Announce launch is coming
- Build anticipation
- No CTA yet

### Open (Day 0)
- Full announcement (template above)
- Clear CTA

### Social proof (Day 2)
- Student stories / testimonials
- Specific results
- CTA

### FAQ / Objections (Day 4)
- Common questions answered
- Overcome hesitations
- CTA

### Final reminder (Day 6)
- Last chance messaging
- Urgency (doors closing)
- Strong CTA

### Closed (Day 7)
- Thank those who joined
- When next intake is
- What to do in meantime

**Note**: Each email in the sequence should feel different from the last. Don't just repeat the same structure with different content. The Day 2 email might be mostly a story. The Day 4 email might be almost entirely Q&A format. The final reminder can be three sentences.

## Voice notes
- More direct than newsletters - there's an ask
- Still personal, not corporate
- Specifics build trust (exact numbers, named companies)
- Urgency must be real, not manufactured
- Offer the "reply for honest advice" out
