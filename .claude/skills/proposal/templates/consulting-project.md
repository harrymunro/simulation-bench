# Template: Consulting Project Proposal

For simulation modelling, AI implementation, technical advisory, and data science engagements delivered through Aspegio.

**Do NOT follow this as a fill-in-the-blanks template.** These are structural starting points. A good proposal feels bespoke. If someone could tell you used a template, you've failed. Read [ANTI_AI_DETECTION.md](../../shared/ANTI_AI_DETECTION.md) first.

## Best rhetoric devices

- **Litotes** for understated credibility ("not unfamiliar with", "not a trivial problem")
- **Antithesis** for current state vs proposed state, or contrasting approaches
- **Anadiplosis** for connecting one section's conclusion to the next section's opening

Use at most 1-2. They must be invisible. A proposal with identifiable rhetorical devices reads as crafted, not authored.

## Approaches

Consulting proposals need to do two things: demonstrate understanding of the problem and make the solution feel inevitable. The structure should serve those goals, not a template. All proposals must use formal, third-person voice throughout. No first person ("I", "my", "me"). Use "Aspegio" or impersonal constructions.

### Approach 1: Problem-first

Lead with your understanding of their situation. Show you listened. Then propose the solution as a natural response to the problem you just described. This works when the client has a clear, well-defined problem and you want to demonstrate you grasped it.

### Approach 2: Solution-first

Lead with what you'll build or deliver. Then explain why that's the right response to their situation. This works when the client already knows they want simulation (or AI, or whatever), and the question is whether you're the right person. Get to "here's what I'd do" fast.

### Approach 3: Story-first

Lead with a relevant past engagement (anonymised). "I worked with a pharmaceutical company that had almost exactly this problem." Then pivot to how you'd adapt that approach for this client. This works when you have a directly relevant case study and the client needs reassurance that you've done this before.

## Loose Structure

```
# [Project Name - descriptive, not generic]

## [Situation understanding]
[Reflect back what they told you. Reference specifics from
the conversation. Show you grasped the real problem, which
might be different from what they initially asked for.]

## [Proposed approach]
[Concrete activities, not methodology.
"Aspegio will build X" not "We'll apply our proprietary framework."
Include what will not be done. Honest scoping builds trust.]

## [Deliverables]
[Concrete list. Each item is a specific thing they'll receive.
Vary the level of detail, some items need a sentence of
explanation, others are self-evident.]

## [Timeline]
[Approximate, not fictional precision. Note dependencies
on client inputs. Mention what you need from them and when.]

## [About / relevant experience]
[Brief. 2-3 relevant engagements, not a full CV. Understated.
Skip this entirely for existing clients who already know Aspegio.]

## [Investment]
[Direct pricing. Options if relevant. Payment terms.
Don't bury this or make them hunt for the number.]

## [Next steps]
[What happens if they say yes. Keep it to 2-3 sentences.]
```

**Important**: The headers above are placeholders. Use descriptive headers that relate to the actual project. "The Coventry Model" is better than "Deliverables." Avoid generic labels where a specific one serves better.

## Anti-patterns

- **Every section the same length.** Real proposals have a long approach section and a short credentials section, or vice versa. Uniform section lengths scream template.
- **Generic situation understanding.** "We understand you face challenges with operational efficiency." That's not understanding, that's a mail merge. Reference the specific thing they said.
- **Methodology descriptions.** "Our approach follows a five-phase methodology: Discover, Design, Develop, Deploy, Debrief." Nobody cares about your methodology. They care about what you'll actually do for them.
- **Padded deliverables.** Don't add "project documentation", "weekly status reports", and "knowledge transfer sessions" to make the list look longer. If the client gets a working simulation model and a clear recommendations report, that's enough.
- **The "Why Aspegio" section.** AI loves to generate a section selling the company. If your proposal is good, the whole thing sells you. A separate sales pitch section undermines it.
- **Perfectly matched problem-solution bullets.** Three problems in section 1, three matching solutions in section 2, in the same order. Real proposals don't have this symmetry.

## Variations

### Short proposal (1-2 pages)

For smaller engagements or follow-on work with existing clients. Skip the credentials. Compress the situation understanding to a paragraph. Go straight to what you'd do and what it costs.

### Phased proposal

When the engagement is large or uncertain, break it into phases with a clear decision point between them. Phase 1 is always well-defined. Phase 2 is outlined but noted as subject to Phase 1 findings. Don't pretend you know exactly what Phase 2 will look like before you've done Phase 1.

### Advisory / retained

For ongoing advisory relationships rather than project work. Focus on availability, scope of topics, and communication cadence rather than specific deliverables. These can be much shorter.

## Example opening paragraphs

### Problem-first opening

```
During our initial discussion, two points stood out. First, the current
simulation takes three days to set up for each new scenario. Second,
the outputs are routinely challenged at board level because the
underlying assumptions are not well understood.

These are connected problems. A model that takes three days to configure
is a model that does not get run often enough. And a model that runs
infrequently does not build the kind of institutional trust where
stakeholders stop questioning the assumptions and start acting on the
results.
```

### Solution-first opening

```
Aspegio proposes to build a Python-based simulation model of the
production line from raw material intake through to finished goods.
The model would run a full year's production in under 60 seconds,
enabling the planning team to test scheduling changes without
dependence on IT.

The rationale for this approach follows from the challenges described
in our initial conversation.
```

### Story-first opening

```
In a recent engagement, Aspegio worked with a chemicals manufacturer
facing a similar challenge. Their batch scheduling relied on
spreadsheets, and every shift in demand patterns required two weeks
of manual schedule testing. The simulation model delivered reduced
that cycle to 30 seconds. The client ran over 200 scenarios in the
first month.

The present situation is not identical, but the core dynamic is
the same.
```
