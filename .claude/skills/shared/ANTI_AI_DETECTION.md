# Writing Like a Human, Not a Language Model

This guide exists because AI-generated content has recognisable patterns. People spot them instantly, especially on Reddit. Every piece of content must pass as something a real person wrote in a text editor, not something generated by a prompt.

**Read this before writing anything. These rules override the templates.**

## The Core Problem

LLMs produce content that is:
- Perfectly structured (uniform paragraph lengths, symmetric arguments, tidy headers)
- Relentlessly balanced (every point gets a counterpoint)
- Smooth (every transition is signposted, every section connects neatly)
- Complete (no loose ends, no tangents, no trailing thoughts)
- Consistent in register (same level of formality throughout)

Real human writing is none of these things. Real writing is messy, opinionated, inconsistent, and full of personality. That's what makes it trustworthy.

## Structure Rules

### Break symmetry deliberately

If you list three points, make them different lengths. One might be a sentence, another a full paragraph, the third just a few words. Never format all bullet points identically.

Bad (AI pattern):
```
- **Point one**: explanation of the first thing in about twelve words
- **Point two**: explanation of the second thing in about twelve words
- **Point three**: explanation of the third thing in about twelve words
```

Good (human pattern):
```
- Point one is straightforward enough
- Point two needs more explanation because it's genuinely complicated and there's a
  gotcha that most people hit when they first encounter it
- And honestly point three might not even belong here but I'm including it anyway
```

### Vary paragraph length wildly

A paragraph can be one word. It can be eight sentences. The key is that the variation looks unplanned.

Don't write five consecutive paragraphs of 2-3 sentences each. That's an AI fingerprint visible from space.

### Don't use headers as an essay outline

Headers like `## The Problem` → `## The Solution` → `## What I Learned` → `## Conclusion` scream AI. Real people use headers inconsistently:
- Sometimes they use them, sometimes they don't
- Sometimes a header is descriptive ("How the queuing model works"), sometimes it's a quip ("The bit where everything broke")
- Long posts might have headers. Short posts probably shouldn't.
- Not every section needs one

### Skip the neat ending

Not every piece needs a tidy wrap-up. Sometimes the best ending is just... stopping. The urge to summarise everything with a bow on top is an AI tell.

## Language Rules

### Sentence fragments are fine

"Worked perfectly. Until it didn't."

Fragments are how people actually write informally. Use them. Not constantly (that becomes its own pattern) but enough to break the monotony of complete sentences.

### Parenthetical asides

Real people go on tangents. Use parentheses for thoughts that don't quite fit but you're including anyway (because that's how brains work, you think of something mid-sentence and just... say it).

### Self-corrections mid-thought

"The model ran in about 30 seconds. Actually no, more like 45 once I added the warm-up period."

This is extremely human. AI almost never does it.

### Vary your register

Go from technical to casual within the same paragraph. A sentence about Poisson distributions followed by "anyway, it works" is more human than maintaining a consistent tone throughout.

### Contractions. Always.

"I would" → "I'd". "It is" → "It's". "Do not" → "Don't". AI tends toward the formal. Real people contract everything in informal writing.

### Start sentences with conjunctions

And, But, So, Or. AI avoids these as sentence starters. Humans use them constantly.

### No em dashes

Em dashes (—) are now a strong AI tell. LLMs overuse them constantly, for asides, mid-sentence pivots, and list explanations. Readers clock them immediately. Never use em dashes in generated content. Use periods, commas, or restructure the sentence instead.

- Bad: "The model worked perfectly — until it didn't."
- Good: "The model worked perfectly. Until it didn't."
- Bad: "Not a theoretical framework — something you actually do."
- Good: "Not a theoretical framework. Something you actually do."

## Grammar-Level Tells

These patterns are harder to spot individually but create a cumulative "AI texture" that readers feel even if they can't name it.

### Participial clause overload

AI uses present participial clauses (phrases starting with -ing words) at 2-5x the rate of human writers. They create an information-dense, noun-heavy style.

Bad (AI pattern):
> Brian, leaning on his agility, dances around the ring, evading Show's heavy blows.

Good (human pattern):
> Brian leans on his agility. He dances around the ring and evades Show's heavy blows.

If you spot more than one participial clause in a sentence, break it up. Use finite verbs instead.

### Nominalization addiction

AI prefers abstract nouns over verbs. "The implementation of the solution" instead of "we implemented it." "The exploration of alternatives" instead of "we explored alternatives."

Use the verb form. "We built it" beats "the construction of it" every time.

### Copulative avoidance

AI avoids simple "X is Y" constructions, replacing them with fancier phrasings. "SimPy is a Python library" is perfectly good English. AI would write "SimPy represents a powerful Python-based simulation framework that enables..."

Simple sentences using "is" and "are" are fine. They're how people actually explain things.

## Punctuation Tells

### Use semicolons and parentheses

AI rarely uses semicolons or parenthetical asides in parentheses. Humans use both freely. A semicolon connecting two related thoughts or a parenthetical tangent (like this one) reads as distinctly human.

### Imperfect punctuation is fine

AI never makes minor punctuation mistakes. It always uses Oxford commas, always closes parentheses, always matches its dashes. Real writing occasionally drops an Oxford comma, uses inconsistent list punctuation, or starts a parenthetical aside and just... moves on without closing it. Don't manufacture errors, but don't polish them out either.

## Tone Tells

### Commit to negative opinions

AI is relentlessly positive, measured, and "helpful." It hedges with both-sides framing instead of taking a stance. Real people have negative opinions and express them directly.

"The documentation is bad" is more human than "while the documentation presents certain challenges, it also offers opportunities for community contribution."

### Express genuine uncertainty

AI avoids admitting it doesn't know something. "I haven't figured this out yet" or "honestly I'm not sure" are distinctly human. So is trailing off mid-thought because you haven't fully formed the idea.

### Disagree directly

AI frames disagreement as "an alternative perspective." Humans say "I think that's wrong" or "no, that's not how it works." Be direct when you disagree. Hedged disagreement reads as generated.

## Banned Phrases and Vocabulary

AI-generated content clusters around specific words and phrases that have become strong tells. The full list is too long to include here (200+ words, 90+ phrases) and is maintained as a dataset for automated checking.

**Common examples** (so you know what to watch for):

Phrases: "Here's the thing", "Let's dive in", "It's worth noting", "At the end of the day", "Curious what others think", "A testament to", "Paving the way for", "The landscape of", "In today's [X]"

Words: delve, tapestry, multifaceted, pivotal, underscore, foster, harness, illuminate, navigate, robust, seamless, comprehensive, holistic, nuanced, transformative, paramount, meticulous

For the comprehensive list and automated checking, see the "Automated Check" section below.

### Replacement approach

Don't replace these with other stock phrases. Instead, just say what you mean directly. If you find yourself reaching for a transition phrase, that's a sign the structure is too rigid. Restructure instead.

## Rhetorical Devices: Invisible or Not At All

The rhetoric selector gives you devices. Here's how to use them without getting caught:

### Use 1-2 devices maximum, not 3

Three rhetorical devices in a single piece makes it feel composed, not written. Pick the one that fits most naturally. Use a second if it genuinely emerges. Skip the third.

### Devices must be invisible to the reader

If someone could circle your tricolon and say "that's a tricolon," you've applied it too heavily. The device should make the writing feel good without being identifiable.

Bad (visible tricolon):
> SimPy is free. SimPy is flexible. SimPy is powerful enough for production use.

Good (invisible tricolon):
> It's free, bends to whatever weird edge case you throw at it, and I've been running it in production for two years.

### Never create perfect parallel structures

If you're using anaphora, break the pattern on the last repetition. If you're using tricolon, make one element significantly longer or shorter than the others. Perfect symmetry is the single biggest AI tell in rhetoric.

### Don't use devices as scaffolding

Rhetorical devices are word-level and sentence-level tools. They should never determine the structure of a post. If your antithesis is becoming your entire argument framework (section 1 vs section 2), you've gone too far.

## Anti-Patterns: What NOT to Do

### The balanced essay

Every argument with a counterargument, every claim with a caveat, every position with "but on the other hand." Real people have opinions. They lean into them. They don't give equal airtime to both sides of everything.

### The manufactured engagement closer

"What do you think? Am I wrong about this? I'd love to hear your perspective."

This is the single most common AI tell in social content. If you want to end with a question, make it specific and genuinely curious:
- Bad: "What are your thoughts on this?"
- Bad: "Curious what others think"
- Better: "Has anyone actually benchmarked FilterStore vs PriorityResource at scale? I couldn't find numbers."
- Best: Just end. Not every post needs a question.

### The TL;DR mini-essay

AI writes TL;DRs that are 3-4 carefully crafted sentences with semicolons and perfect punctuation. Real TL;DRs are lazy:
- "TL;DR: use FilterStore, not Resource, if your queue items aren't identical"
- "TL;DR simpy is free and does 80% of what Arena does"
- Just one sentence. Maybe two if you're feeling generous.

### The uniform bullet point

Every bullet starting with **bold text** followed by a colon or dash followed by an explanation of similar length. Real bullet points are messy:
- Some are short
- Some are long and go into detail because the point genuinely needs it and you can't really compress it without losing the nuance, which would defeat the purpose
- Some don't even need to be bullet points honestly
- Occasional ones are just a link or a code snippet

### The smooth transition

"Now let's look at..." / "Building on this..." / "With that in mind..."

Real people don't signpost every section change. Sometimes you just start talking about the next thing. The reader will keep up.

### The complete thought

AI finishes every thought neatly. Real writing sometimes just... doesn't. A half-formed idea followed by "but I haven't fully worked this out" is more human than a perfectly closed argument.

## Platform-Specific Notes

### Reddit (strictest)
- People actively look for AI content to downvote
- Roughness is authenticity. Polish is suspicious.
- Code and specific numbers are your credibility, not prose quality
- If your post reads like an essay, rewrite it to read like a forum comment

### LinkedIn (moderate)
- More polish acceptable, but the patterns still get called out
- The hook-body-CTA structure is fine but vary how you execute it
- Break the "short paragraphs with line breaks" pattern occasionally
- Your opinion should be clearly yours, not a balanced take

### Articles/Blog (most forgiving)
- More structure is natural for long-form
- But still break the essay-outline pattern
- Tangents and asides are expected in blog writing
- Personal anecdotes should feel spontaneous, not narratively convenient

### Email (most forgiving)
- Conversational tone expected
- Structure matters less, people skim
- The real risk is the subject line sounding generated
- P.S. lines are naturally human. Use them.

## Automated Check

After writing any content and saving it to a file, run the AI writing checker:

```bash
uv run python .claude/skills/shared/scripts/ai_writing_checker.py <file_path>
```

The script checks for:
1. **Banned vocabulary** (200+ AI-overused words)
2. **Banned phrases** (90+ AI-tell phrases)
3. **Structural uniformity** (paragraph lengths too similar)
4. **Em dashes** (strong AI tell)
5. **Rule of three** (compulsive tricolon patterns)
6. **Negative parallelism** ("not just X, but also Y")
7. **Transition word density** (moreover, furthermore, additionally...)

It outputs a JSON report with a score. **A score of 0 is the target.** Fix all `flag` severity items. Fix `warning` items where possible. `info` items are advisory.

**The fix loop:**
1. Run the checker on your saved file
2. Read the JSON output
3. Fix each flagged item in the content
4. Re-run the checker
5. Repeat until score is 0

The checker loads its vocabulary from `.claude/skills/shared/data/ai_vocabulary.json`. The dataset can be updated independently of this guide.

## The Litmus Test

Before finalising any content, ask:

1. **Could I identify this as AI if I saw it in the wild?** If yes, rewrite.
2. **Is the structure too neat?** If you could outline it in 30 seconds, it's too neat.
3. **Does every paragraph serve the argument?** If yes, you need a tangent.
4. **Is the ending tidy?** If yes, consider just stopping earlier.
5. **Would I actually write this in a text editor at 11pm?** If it's too polished for that, strip it back.
