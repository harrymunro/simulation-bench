# Rhetorical Devices Reference

From Mark Forsyth's "The Elements of Eloquence" - techniques to make phrases striking and memorable.

---

## Tricolon (Rule of Three)

**Definition**: Three parallel elements of similar length and structure.

**Why it works**: The human brain finds patterns of three satisfying and complete.

**Examples**:
- "Veni, vidi, vici" (I came, I saw, I conquered)
- "Blood, sweat, and tears"
- "Learn it. Build it. Ship it."

**In your content**:
- "SimPy is free, flexible, and fast."
- "I wrote the code, ran the simulation, and found the bug in ten minutes."
- "No licence fees. No vendor lock-in. No excuses."

---

## Antithesis (Contrasting Ideas)

**Definition**: Placing opposing ideas in parallel structure for emphasis.

**Why it works**: Contrast makes both sides clearer and more memorable.

**Examples**:
- "Ask not what your country can do for you - ask what you can do for your country"
- "One small step for man, one giant leap for mankind"

**In your content**:
- "Commercial software costs thousands; SimPy costs nothing."
- "They charge for support; the community gives it freely."
- "I used to wrestle with the tool. Now the tool works for me."

---

## Anaphora (Repetition at Start)

**Definition**: Beginning successive phrases or sentences with the same word(s).

**Why it works**: Creates rhythm, builds momentum, drives a point home.

**Examples**:
- "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields..."
- "I have a dream... I have a dream..."

**In your content**:
- "You don't need a consultant. You don't need a training budget. You don't need permission."
- "Every time I opened the software, I paid. Every time I added a user, I paid. Every time I needed support, I paid."

---

## Epistrophe (Repetition at End)

**Definition**: Ending successive phrases with the same word(s).

**Why it works**: Creates a powerful conclusion that echoes.

**Examples**:
- "Government of the people, by the people, for the people"
- "When I was a child, I spoke as a child, I understood as a child, I thought as a child"

**In your content**:
- "The old way costs more. The old way takes longer. The old way keeps you stuck."
- "They said it couldn't be done in Python. I did it in Python."

---

## Anadiplosis (End Becomes Beginning)

**Definition**: The last word of one phrase becomes the first word of the next.

**Why it works**: Creates a chain of logic, builds momentum through connection.

**Examples**:
- "Fear leads to anger. Anger leads to hate. Hate leads to suffering."
- "Work to live, live to love, love to work."

**In your content**:
- "Understanding leads to confidence. Confidence leads to action. Action leads to results."
- "The simulation showed a bottleneck. The bottleneck was in receiving. Receiving needed two more staff."

---

## Hyperbaton (Unusual Word Order)

**Definition**: Deliberately disrupting normal sentence structure.

**Why it works**: Unexpected order catches attention, emphasises key words.

**Examples**:
- "This I must see" (instead of "I must see this")
- "Powerful you have become" - Yoda

**In your content**:
- "Free, it costs you nothing." (emphasis on FREE)
- "Wrong, I was. Completely wrong."
- "Elegant, the solution wasn't. But it worked."

---

## Chiasmus (Reversed Structure)

**Definition**: Reversing the structure of two parallel phrases (AB-BA pattern).

**Why it works**: Creates a satisfying sense of balance and completeness.

**Examples**:
- "Ask not what your country can do for you, ask what you can do for your country"
- "When the going gets tough, the tough get going"

**In your content**:
- "Don't learn to use the tool - use the tool to learn."
- "I stopped waiting for permission and started asking for forgiveness."

---

## Polyptoton (Same Root, Different Forms)

**Definition**: Repeating words derived from the same root in different forms.

**Why it works**: Creates wordplay that's clever without being forced.

**Examples**:
- "The only thing we have to fear is fear itself"
- "Judge not, lest ye be judged"

**In your content**:
- "I simulated the simulation before I ran the simulation."
- "Teaching teaches the teacher."
- "The model modelled what we couldn't model before."

---

## Asyndeton (Omitting Conjunctions)

**Definition**: Leaving out conjunctions between words or phrases (no "and").

**Why it works**: Creates speed, urgency, impact.

**Examples**:
- "I came, I saw, I conquered" (not "I came, I saw, AND I conquered")
- "Reduce, reuse, recycle"

**In your content**:
- "Install Python. Import SimPy. Build your first model."
- "No licence. No subscription. No vendor calls."

---

## Polysyndeton (Adding Conjunctions)

**Definition**: Using more conjunctions than necessary (extra "and"s).

**Why it works**: Creates a sense of accumulation, exhaustiveness, emphasis.

**Examples**:
- "And the rain descended, and the floods came, and the winds blew"

**In your content**:
- "And then you need to export the data. And then you need to clean it. And then you need to visualise it. And then..."
- "I tried Arena. And Simul8. And FlexSim. And AnyLogic."

---

## Hyperbole (Deliberate Exaggeration)

**Definition**: Extravagant overstatement not meant literally.

**Why it works**: Creates emphasis through obvious exaggeration (British humour loves this).

**Examples**:
- "I've told you a million times"
- "This bag weighs a ton"

**In your content**:
- "I've probably spent roughly a thousand hours fighting software licences."
- "The documentation was so thin you could read it during a sneeze."

---

## Litotes (Understated Negation)

**Definition**: Affirming something by denying its opposite (very British).

**Why it works**: Creates dry understatement, sounds modest while making a point.

**Examples**:
- "Not bad" (meaning "good")
- "He's not the sharpest tool in the shed"

**In your content**:
- "The results were not entirely disappointing." (meaning: they were great)
- "The software wasn't exactly cheap." (meaning: it was outrageously expensive)
- "I'm not saying it's easy. I'm saying it's possible."

---

## Usage Guidelines

1. **Don't force it** - If a device doesn't fit naturally, don't use it
2. **One or two per piece** - Overuse dilutes impact
3. **Match device to content type**:
   - Technical: Tricolon, Asyndeton (clean, direct)
   - Provocative: Antithesis, Anaphora (builds argument)
   - Personal: Anadiplosis, Hyperbaton (creates narrative flow)
4. **Litotes is your secret weapon** - Very British, very you
